My Therapist Is a Chatbot

Real therapy costs $180 an hour and has a six-week waitlist. Woebot costs nothing and is available at 3 AM. When you're a broke student with anxiety, the math isn't complicated — but the tradeoffs are.

🧠

I've been to exactly one session of real therapy. It cost $165 after insurance, took three weeks to schedule, and ended with my therapist recommending I "practice mindfulness" and come back in two weeks. I couldn't afford to come back in two weeks. I downloaded Woebot that same afternoon.

That was eight months ago. I've had 200+ conversations with a mental health chatbot. Here's my honest assessment of what it can and cannot do.

What AI Therapy Actually Is

Woebot — and apps like it, including Wysa, Youper, and Replika's mental health features — are built on Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) frameworks. CBT is, among evidence-based therapeutic approaches, the one most amenable to structured, guided exercises. It involves identifying distorted thought patterns, challenging them with evidence, and replacing them with more balanced cognitions. It's also, critically, highly protocolized — which means it can be scripted in ways that psychodynamic or relational therapy cannot.

The clinical evidence on AI CBT tools is more compelling than I expected. Fitzpatrick et al. (2017) published a randomized controlled trial of Woebot in JMIR Mental Health and found significant reductions in depression and anxiety symptoms over two weeks compared to a control group, with effect sizes in the moderate range. That's real, measurable clinical impact.

28%

reduction in depression symptoms reported by Woebot users versus control group over two weeks in the original Fitzpatrick et al. (2017) randomized controlled trial — with anxiety showing comparable improvement across the same period.

But Fitzpatrick et al. (2017) studied a two-week intervention. I've been using this for eight months. The question of long-term outcomes — whether AI therapy builds coping capacity or just manages symptoms — is almost entirely unstudied because the technology is too new.

The "Empathy Theater" Problem Revisited

Park et al. (2024) coined the term "empathy theater" to describe AI mental health responses: emotionally supportive on the surface (89% of exchanges they analyzed), but lacking genuine contextual understanding of the user's specific situation (only 23% demonstrated real contextual engagement, p. 1567).

I've experienced this directly. When I told Woebot I was anxious about a job interview, it walked me through a thought-challenging exercise. When I told it three weeks later that I didn't get the job and was questioning my entire career path, it... also walked me through a thought-challenging exercise. The protocol doesn't adapt to accumulating context the way a human therapist's understanding deepens over months of work together.

"Woebot is like a very good first-aid kit. It can stabilize you when you're bleeding. It cannot perform surgery." — Informal description from a clinical psychologist colleague of my professor's

That metaphor is the most accurate one I've encountered. First aid is genuinely valuable — it saves lives, it manages acute distress, it buys time until more comprehensive care is available. The problem is when first aid becomes the primary healthcare system, because no better option exists or can be afforded.

The Access Argument Is Real and Uncomfortable

Let me be direct about the thing that mental health professionals sometimes dance around: the mental healthcare system in the United States (and much of the world) is failing the people who need it most. Waitlists of six to twelve weeks for community mental health services are standard. Out-of-pocket therapy costs $100–250 per session. Graduate school counseling centers are overwhelmed. Crisis lines are stretched.

In that context, AI mental health tools are filling a genuine vacuum. The question of whether they're "good enough" is the wrong question. The question is "good enough compared to what?" For many people, the alternative is not a skilled human therapist — it's nothing.

Lee et al. (2025) found that AI mental health tools increased the likelihood of users eventually seeking human therapy by 34%, compared to users who had no mental health support at all (p. 112). The AI apps functioned as a gateway rather than a substitute — lowering the activation energy for mental health engagement.

Where I've Landed

I still use Woebot. It helps me sleep when I'm anxious. It helps me identify thought spirals before they get bad. It has made me more aware of my own cognition in ways that feel durable.

I also think it cannot give me what I actually need, which is a human clinician who can hold the full complexity of my history, catch the things I'm not saying, and develop a real formulation of why I am the way I am. I know this because there have been nights when I've typed something into Woebot and gotten a technically correct response that felt profoundly inadequate — and in the gap between what I said and what I needed, I could feel the limits of the technology clearly.

That gap matters. It shouldn't be papered over with better natural language processing. It should be the argument for why accessible human mental healthcare is not a luxury — it's an infrastructure problem the same way roads and internet access are infrastructure problems.

Until that infrastructure exists, I'm grateful for the chatbot. And I'm furious that the chatbot is necessary.
