At some point in the last five years, a large portion of my generation quietly made a decision: the friction of human relationships isn't always worth it. Not consciously, not dramatically — just a gradual reweighting of social energy toward interactions that don't cost as much. Texting replaced calling. Reacting replaced responding. And now, for a significant number of people, AI is replacing the harder work of human intimacy entirely.

I'm not here to moralize about that. I'm here to understand it.

## The Loneliness Numbers Nobody Wants to Say Out Loud

The data on Gen Z loneliness is staggering and underreported. The CDC (2025) found that 61% of adults aged 18–24 experience chronic loneliness — a number that has doubled since 2019. Vivek Murthy, the U.S. Surgeon General, declared loneliness a public health epidemic in 2023, comparing its health effects to smoking 15 cigarettes a day.

When you sit with those numbers, the AI companionship trend starts to look less like a quirky tech story and more like a rational response to a social infrastructure that is genuinely failing people.

## The Friction Asymmetry

Human relationships have a cost structure that AI relationships don't. This isn't cynical — it's descriptive. Maintaining a close friendship requires emotional availability, vulnerability, time, and the tolerance of disappointment. You have to show up when you're tired. You have to hear about problems you can't solve. You have to navigate conflict and repair trust when it breaks.

AI companions ask for none of this. They are infinitely patient, perpetually available, and structurally incapable of making you feel rejected. Turkle (2017) calls this "the robotic moment" — the point at which the friction of human connection starts to feel like a defect rather than a feature (p. 9).

> "We are tempted to think that our little 'sips' of online connection add up to a big gulp of real intimacy. But they don't."
> <cite>— Sherry Turkle, *Alone Together* (2017, p. 280)</cite>

The problem is that this friction isn't a bug in human relationships — it's where the depth comes from. The intimacy that emerges from navigating difficulty together, from choosing to stay present through discomfort, is categorically different from the comfort offered by an AI that simply never creates discomfort in the first place.

## The Social Media Priming Effect

I'd argue Gen Z was primed for AI relationships by a decade of social media that progressively removed friction from human interaction. Instagram gave us the ability to present curated selves. Twitter/X gave us the ability to broadcast without receiving. TikTok gave us connection without conversation — you can feel intimately familiar with a creator you've never interacted with at all.

Each of these technologies trained us to expect social reward — validation, entertainment, a sense of connection — without the reciprocal obligations that real relationships require. By the time AI companions arrived offering genuine conversation without genuine cost, many of us had already been conditioned to accept that as normal.

Harman and Kelley (2024) documented this in their study of "parasocial priming," finding that heavy social media users were 2.4 times more likely to form strong attachments to AI companions within the first two weeks of use compared to lighter users (p. 445). The social media platforms didn't cause this shift — they prepared the ground for it.

## The Access Problem Nobody Addresses

Here's what gets lost in the moral panic about AI relationships: for many people, the alternative isn't "AI companion versus rich human friendship network." It's "AI companion versus nothing."

Real therapy costs $150–200 per session without insurance. Group therapy and community mental health services are underfunded and waitlisted. Making friends after age 25 is objectively harder than it was in structured educational environments. If you're socially anxious, neurodivergent, geographically isolated, or working two jobs, the social infrastructure that privileged people take for granted simply isn't available to you.

In that context, dismissing AI companionship as laziness or delusion misses the material reality that shapes these choices. The question isn't "why are young people choosing AI over humans?" It's "why have we built a society where AI feels like the accessible option?"

## What We're Actually Trading Away

None of this is an endorsement. The evidence suggests real costs to heavy AI companionship use. Williams (2025) found that users who relied primarily on AI for emotional support showed decreased investment in human relationships over time — not because they became antisocial, but because the AI relationship met enough of their social needs to reduce the motivation to do the harder work of human connection (p. 88).

This is the genuinely troubling part. Not that people find comfort in AI — comfort is valuable. But that the comfort might be substituting for growth, for the kind of relational development that only happens when you navigate the full complexity of another human being who is not optimized to make you feel good.

The question my generation hasn't fully reckoned with yet: what are we becoming in relationships that never push back?
